Throughout my time at West Virginia University (WVU) I discovered, among many other things, my interest in robotics.

## Freshman Year

In my freshman year, I was hungry for skills and wanted to become a useful engineer. I attended manufacturing workshops held by my school's maker space that covered various manufacturing and design topics. After an evening at a soldering workshop in February 2022, I rushed back to my dorm to try to fill a to-go box at the dining hall before it closed.

Illuminated in the wide campus sidewalks was a lone robot. Intrigued, I stopped to observe. Turning the corner, I found two people who would engender my deep interest in robotics. Stephen and Gavin. They were conducting some field testing of a new iteration of their robot. I expected to stop for a moment, but instead spent hours talking about the [University Rover Challenge](https://urc.marssociety.org/), the new robot they were testing, and how I can learn and contribute.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/pathfinder.jpg)

The next day, I joined the WVU University Rover Challenge (URC) Team as a volunteer programmer and started attending meetings and coding sessions. With no prior experience in robotics, and no experience developing software on a team, I was initially intimidated. 

The team and team advisor, [Dr. Yu Gu](https://directory.statler.wvu.edu/faculty-staff-directory/yu-gu), utilize a trial by fire approach where exploration is encouraged and failure is an expected and treasured obligation. This is an approach that I continue to appreciate.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/urc_group_photo.jpg)

Slowly, I was able to build up a modest collection of small programs utilizing the Robot Operating System (ROS). Becoming more confident, my next target was contributing to that year's robot. My first real task on the team was developing the robot's feedback system for path following. 

After working with experienced programmers, debugging failed iterations, and pouring over a massive amount of forum posts and documentation, the system finally worked. This experience strengthened my interest in robotics, and entrench my appreciation for reading documentation. I was invigorated and wanted to learn more.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/loopy.png)

In May of 2022, I started conducting research on [swarm robotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/swarm_robotics) for Dr. Gu at the WVU [Interactive Robotics Laboratory](https://web.archive.org/web/20250423170205/https://yugu.faculty.wvu.edu/people). The research surrounded a robot named Loopy. Loopy consists of a closed chain of many servo motors acting as independent agents that have only a single degree of freedom. 

The preliminary research was focused on consensus methods in swarm robotics and how they can be applied alongside human interaction with Loopy to perform decentralized shape creation.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/loopy_symposium.jpg)

## Sophomore Year

Throughout my sophomore year, myself and graduate students in the lab continued exploring various swarm robotics concepts including how [turing patterns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turing_pattern) can enable loopy to morph or undergo transformation. 

I continued volunteering and started as the programming lead for the WVU URC team. In this role I helped lead 20 programmers in designing and testing an autonomy system, achieving a [first place victory](https://www.marssociety.org/news/2023/06/05/team-mountaineers-crowned-urc2023-champion/) in the 2023 competition.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/mock_competition.jpg)

In the summer, I continued working at the Interactive Robotics Laboratory on a different project. This new project was focused on developing a tool for creating a real-time safety map of retail spaces utilizing a ground-based robot. This research included collaboration with the University of Florida and funding from the National Science Foundation.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/irl_group_photo.jpg)

## Junior Year

In October 2023, I attended the IEEE/RSJ International Conference on Intelligent Robots and Systems in Detroit, Michigan, where my graduate student colleagues and I presented our prior research on Loopy.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/iros.jpg)

Now in my third year of URC, I created the algorithms subteam to allow URC members interested in robotics to deeply explore specific subject areas. With a focus on bringing state of the art robotics algorithms and concepts to our team, the goal of this subteam was to allow the abilities of our robot to mature and keep pace with current industry and academia while encouraging exploration.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/rock_garden.jpg)

Once the new 2024 URC rules were released, our team began development on our new competition robot. Designs were finalized and parts were ordered while new sensors, path planning algorithms, and autonomy systems were tested on robots from previous years. During this period, our team also held outreach events for new students interested in joining the team.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/outreach.jpg)

After winter break ended and all of our parts were delivered, we assembled the new robot and began on software development, integration, and testing. With a transition from different major ROS versions, our team was faced with tight development deadlines in a stressful but rewarding period. Our software stack was entirely overhauled changing everything from device drivers to our robot's comms system.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/heimdall_comms_debugging.jpg)

Ultimately after months of integration and testing, our team submitted our system acceptance review to the competition judges, and was selected to compete in Hanksville, Utah at the Mars Desert Research Station just outside of Capitol Reef National Park.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/cliff_face.jpg)

Throughout our week traveling and competing, our team spent evenings at our rental home preparing for missions, practicing setup procedures, and running through different failure recovery plans.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/utah_testing.jpg)

During the autonomy mission, we readied in the basestation, prepared the autonomy system, and sat back and watched our robot's performance. Ultimately, we were able to place second in the mission, with our robot having successfully traversed the rocky and unforgiving environment to reach the mission goals.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/basestation.jpg)

After completing all of the missions in the competition, our scores were totalled by the judges. We had placed [second overall](https://www.marssociety.org/news/2024/06/02/agh-space-systems-crowned-urc-2024-champion/) in the competition.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/autonomy.jpg)

Proud of our accomplishment and the planning, developing, and testing that preceded the competition, we celebrated. We celebrated not only our placement in the competition, but also the incredible team dynamic we were able to create and refine.

![]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/competition_group.jpg)
